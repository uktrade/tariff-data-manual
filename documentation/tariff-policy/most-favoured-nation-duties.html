    What is the Most Favoured Nation principle?

    • Under the WTO agreements, countries cannot normally discriminate between their trading partners. Grant someone a special favour (such as a lower customs duty rate for one of their products) and you have to do the same for all other WTO members.
    • MFN duties are those that are applied globally without favour or privilege.

    Why ‘most-favoured’?

    • This sounds like a contradiction. It suggests special treatment, but in the WTO it actually means non-discrimination — treating virtually everyone equally. Each member treats all the other members equally as “most-favoured” trading partners.
    • If a country improves the benefits that it gives to one trading partner, it has to give the same “best” treatment to all the other WTO members so that they all remain “most-favoured”.
    • There are exceptions …
      • countries can set up a free trade agreement that applies only to goods traded within the group
      • they can give developing countries special access to their markets GSP.
      • a country can raise barriers against products that are considered to be traded unfairly from specific countries.
    • But the agreements only permit these exceptions under strict conditions.
    • In general, MFN means that every time a country lowers a trade barrier or opens up a market, it has to do so for the same goods or services from all its trading partners — whether rich or poor, weak or strong.

    MFN - universal and the lowest common denominator

    • The MFN is unique, inasmuch as there must be coverage of the entire tariff with one of two measure types (or both):
      • 103 (Third country duty)
      • 105 (Non preferential duty under end-use)
    • There are c. 24,500 commodity codes on the Tariff, of which c. 16,500 are declarable end-lines – every single one of these end-lines has an MFN duty against it
    • In a lot of cases, the MFN rate is 0%, but that 0% still needs to be actively set against the commodity.
    • The MFN is what will be charged unless the trader actively takes steps to take advantage of a more preferential rate, such as a tariff preference associated with a Free Trade Agreement or a quota.
    • It’s vital when we come to migrate data for the UK’s own MFN after the end of the Implementation Period that coverage of the commodity code base is complete (103s and 105s).

    Erga Omnes

    • Erga Omnes is a Latin phrase which means “towards all” or “towards everyone”. In trade terms, Erga Omnes is the geographical area group which covers the entire world
    • All MFN duties (103s and 105s) are created against Erga Omnes, as are the majority of global import measures.
    • Erga Omnes has a geographical area ID of 1011.
    • There is an additional country group entitled “All third countries”, with ID 1008, which is used almost identically to Erga Omnes – they are practically interchangeable.
    • The only difference between the two is that 1011 contains the European Union as a country, whereas this is excluded from 1008.
    • There are no hard and fast rules about which should be used: Erga Omnes is used more for import measures and All third countries more for export measures, but this is by no means hard and fast.

    Authorised use (End use)

    What is authorised use?

    Goods imported into the EU are regulated by a range of measures aimed at giving EU and UK businesses an equal footing with businesses elsewhere in the world. One of these is End-Use Relief (will be Authorised Use Relief).

    This allows a reduced or zero rate of customs duty on some goods when used for specific purposes and within a set time period. End-Use Relief does not apply to VAT or excise duties.

    End-Use Relief is available on certain goods imported into the EU that are to be put to a specific use within the customs territory of EU. To be eligible for End-Use Relief, products must meet defined criteria and be identified in the Trade Tariff.

    End-use goods include:

    • shipwork goods
    • aircraft and parts
    • hydrocarbon oil
    • marine propulsion engines
    • military equipment
    • fish
    • cheese
    • casein (used in the cheese industry)

    Determining what goods are for ‘authorised use’

    • Sadly, there is nothing so simple as a flag on a commodity code to define it is intended for authorised use purposes. There are commodity codes which are:
      • exclusively provisioned to be used for authorised use
      • under certain circumstances available for authorised use
    • The simplest way to find a commodity that is designated solely for authorised use is to check for commodities that have measure type 105 (Non preferential duty under end-use) instead of 103 (Third country duty) against them. This is a small faction of the total number of commodities in the hierarchy.
    • In addition, the descriptions will have pointers to their end-use nature, e.g. containing words such as “for use in" or “for the manufacture of” etc.
    • As always, there are exceptions: there are some commodities which have both a 103 and a 105 duty against them, as follows – there are just 5:

    A trader is required to produce certificate N990 to take advantage of authorised use relief. This certificate should be present as a condition on all 105 duties (this also applies to other end-use measures). N990 is currently called “EUS - Authorisation for the use of end use procedure (Column 8c, Annex A of Delegated Regulation (EU) 2015/2446)”.
