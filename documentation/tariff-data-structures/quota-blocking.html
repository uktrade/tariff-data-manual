Quota blocking

  • Why are quotas blocked?
  • Quota blocking reasons

Unlike suspensions, blocks don’t prevent declarations being made against a quota, instead they temporarily block quota allocations from being taken, so demand for a quota can be assessed. Quota blocking periods are applicable only to First Come First Serve quotas.

Please read the discussion on blocking quotas before reading this - the introduction to quotas is useful and the concepts are very similar.

Why are quotas blocked?

  • Quota blocks are placed on quotas for a limited period of time to prevent allocations from being taken.
  • Unlike suspensions, which effectively invalidate a quota (as if it does not exist and is suspended in law), all a blocking period does is to prevent allocations from being made: it does not prevent declarations from being made against a quota: it just postpones any allocation decision.
  • A quota blocking period usually coincides with the start of a definition period, though not always; for instance, a common blocking reason is to protect the re-opening of a quota after significant returns
  • This effect of a quota blocking period is to give HMRC an opportunity to assess the demand for a quota and potentially give an opportunity for them to make subjective decisions on allocations, e.g. to prevent a single large business from using up an entire allocation before smaller players have got their act together.
  • 277 of the 455 blocking periods created in 2019 coincided with the start of definition period.
  • In these cases, it is most likely that the block will last c. 2 or 3 weeks

Logical model of a quota blocking period

A quota starts on Jan 1st and runs throughout the year The decision is taken to place a block on the quota for the first 15 days, to allow HMRC to make an assessment on potential usage of the quota, by taking allocation requests, but not actually allocating / decrementing the opening balance This gives smaller traders and new entrants an opportunity to take advantage of the reduced rate

Simplified quotas data model (as applies to blocking periods)

Quota blocking data object in detail

As with suspension periods, the quota blocking object is not one of the more complex objects in the database: there are few fields, in fact there are only three that you need to actively define.


Field
Description

quota_blocking_period_sid
Unique identifier, automatically generated by the application when a new suspension period is created – you do not create this yourself

quota_definition_sid
The quota definition to which the suspension is to be attached – you do not need to enter the ID of the definition: you select it from a list (as will be demonstrated)

blocking_start_date
The start date of the suspension blocking period – i.e. 00:00 at the start of the chosen day

blocking_end_date
The end date of the suspension blocking period – i.e. 00:00 at the end of the chosen day

blocking_periods_type
One of the 8 options noted previously – largely options 1 or 3

description
An optional field, up to 500 characters in length, that helps to define the blocking period and the reasons for it.

A blocking period must fit within a definition period

The critical point is that a quota blocking period is defined against a quota definition period: the blocking period MUST NOT straddle the boundary of the definition – in the rare occasion you need to straddle a boundary, create 2 blocking periods
